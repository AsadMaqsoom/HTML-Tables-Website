Treasures of the Boy King

Today marks the 100th anniversary of the opening of King Tutankhamun’s mummy case. A ruler of Egypt in the 14th century BCE, “Tut” became king at the age of 9 and died at 19. His burial chamber and its treasures captured the world’s imagination, a fascination heightened by a museum tour in the 1970s. Yet for centuries, he was an afterthought. The last of his dynasty, his name was stricken from the royal lists by the next dynasty, so the tomb’s location was forgotten until its 1922 discovery.

What’s in a Brand Name?

Bayer trademarked the name Aspirin, for its drug made from acetylsalicyclic, 125 years ago today. That got us thinking about other products—including popsicles, bubble wrap, kerosene, dumpsters, and velcro—that became household names to the point of ubiquity, replacing the original term. Even the word “zipper” was a brand name once. Here are a few more commonly used terms that started life with a price-tag.

Health and wellness

Tylenol is often used as short-hand for its generic alias of acetaminophen, while brands such as Xanax (alprazolam), Viagra (sildenafil) and Prozac (fluoxetine), became synonymous with their chemical names. Other popular health-and-wellness brands that evolved into everyday words include Band-Aid, trademarked by Johnson and Johnson in 1920; the Q-tip, introduced in 1926; and Kleenex.

Fun and games

Products aimed at children tend to have fun names—“Hula Hoop” sounds a lot better than “extruded plastic dingus.” The toy company WHAM-O gave us that one, as well as the Frisbee. Other fun product names that entered the lexicon include yo-yo, pogo stick, ping-pong, rollerblade, and jet ski.

Google, Photoshop, and Taser are a few high-profile examples of brand names that became verbs, just as “to Xerox” once meant to make a copy of something. We also use the words jumbotron and teleprompter interchangeably with the electronic products they represent, though they were product names.
